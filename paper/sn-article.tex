\abstract{
Accurate and interpretable prediction of Vickers hardness is important for analysing graphene-added Si$_3$N$_4$ ceramics, where experimental characterisation is resource-intensive, and datasets are typically small. This study presents an interpretable machine learning framework based on a tuned RuleFit model applied to a dataset of 82 experimentally characterised samples compiled from the literature. Multiple model configurations are benchmarked --- XGBoost with original and engineered features, ElasticNet/linear regularised baselines, Few-Shot KNN, a small deep neural network, and RuleFit --- under fixed 80/20 and 70/30 train--test splits and a 30-replicate stability analysis. On the fixed 70/30 split, RuleFit (Tuned) obtains the best split-specific performance (R$^2 = 0.854$, RMSE~$= 1.955$~GPa, MAE~$= 1.477$~GPa). Across 30 random splits, its performance is comparable to XGBoost, with no statistically significant advantage over the strongest black-box baselines, while it significantly outperforms Few-Shot KNN ($p < 0.001$). Feature importance and explanation analyses identify graphene content/type, sintering pressure, and relative density as key predictors, consistent with ceramic processing--property relationships. Case studies of five test samples show that RuleFit's activated rules provide sample-level explanations that are broadly consistent with SHAP and LIME. The results indicate that rule-based interpretable models can provide competitive accuracy and transparent decision logic for small ceramic datasets, while highlighting the need for leakage-free preprocessing, careful validation, and external experimental testing before deployment for materials design.
}

\section{Introduction}

Silicon nitride (Si$_3$N$_4$) ceramics are among the most widely studied advanced structural ceramics owing to their exceptional combination of mechanical properties: high hardness, superior fracture toughness, good thermal shock resistance, and chemical stability at elevated temperatures \cite{govindasamy2024role}. These characteristics make Si$_3$N$_4$ an attractive material for applications in cutting tools, bearing components, automotive engine parts, and high-temperature structural elements. Among these properties, Vickers hardness is a primary indicator of wear resistance and load-bearing capacity, and its reliable quantification is essential for quality control and material qualification in industrial settings.

The incorporation of graphene and graphene-derived nanomaterials (graphene nanoplatelets, reduced graphene oxide, few-layer graphene) as reinforcing additives in Si$_3$N$_4$ matrices has attracted considerable research interest over the past decade. Graphene additions can modify hardness through multiple competing mechanisms: at low concentrations, well-dispersed graphene platelets may act as crack deflectors, while at higher concentrations or with suboptimal sintering parameters, graphene tends to agglomerate and reduce densification, ultimately lowering hardness. The interplay between graphene type, weight fraction, sintering temperature, sintering pressure, and the resulting relative density creates a complex, high-dimensional process--property space that is difficult to navigate through conventional experimental trial-and-error.

Experimental determination of Vickers hardness requires fabrication of sintered ceramic samples --- a process that involves powder mixing, milling, and high-temperature sintering under controlled pressure --- followed by indentation testing under standardised load conditions. For a dataset spanning the full design space of graphene-added Si$_3$N$_4$ ceramics, this represents a significant investment of time and resources. Machine learning (ML) can therefore support exploratory screening and post-synthesis property analysis by learning relationships between processing, composition, microstructural descriptors, and hardness. Because density and phase composition are post-sintering descriptors, predictions based on the full feature set should be interpreted as a post-synthesis or process--structure--property model rather than a purely pre-synthesis design model. Recent work by Qadir et al.~\cite{qadir2024predicting} demonstrated that gradient-boosted tree models (XGBoost) can predict Vickers hardness on a dataset of 82 experimentally characterised graphene-added Si$_3$N$_4$ ceramics with high accuracy (R$^2 > 0.81$), establishing a strong baseline for the field.

Despite the predictive success of black-box models such as XGBoost and deep neural networks, their lack of interpretability limits their utility for materials design: they do not directly reveal which processing variables drive a particular hardness outcome or under what conditions a high-hardness sample is achievable. Interpretable models that expose explicit decision rules are therefore of practical value, particularly in the context of small datasets where overfitting risk is high and experimental validation of model recommendations is costly. The RuleFit algorithm~\cite{friedman2008predictive} addresses this need by combining gradient-boosted tree ensembles with a sparse linear model, producing human-readable rules of the form ``if Sintering Pressure $>$ 27.5~MPa and Graphene~$\leq$~2.0~wt.\%~then hardness increases by $+X$~GPa''.

In this study, we present an interpretable hardness prediction framework for graphene-added Si$_3$N$_4$ ceramics. A tuned RuleFit model is compared against five baseline model families --- two XGBoost variants, ElasticNet/linear baselines, Few-Shot KNN, and a small deep neural network --- under fixed train--test splits and a 30-replicate stability analysis. Three complementary explanation methods (RuleFit rule activations, SHAP, and LIME) are applied to assess whether the framework provides physically meaningful, sample-level interpretability without a substantial loss in predictive accuracy. The Python code, cleaned dataset, preprocessing scripts, model-training scripts, and random seeds will be deposited in a manuscript-specific repository before publication to support reproducibility.

\section{Materials and Methods}





\subsection{Development of the Interpretable Hardness Prediction Framework}

The dataset used in this study consists of 82 experimental observations of graphene-added Si$_3$N$_4$ ceramics compiled from the literature \cite{qadir2024predicting}. The target variable is Vickers hardness measured in GPa, ranging from 0.46 to 27.00~GPa with a mean of 14.71~GPa. Each sample is described by 21 input features covering graphene properties (type, weight fraction, thickness, surface area), sintering additive characteristics (type and content of two additives), milling conditions (type and duration), sintering parameters (technique, temperature, time, pressure), and post-sintering properties (density, $\alpha$- and $\beta$-Si$_3$N$_4$ phase content, and indentation load).

Categorical features (type of graphene, type of sintering additive 1 and 2, milling type, and sintering technique) were converted into numeric representations using target encoding. To minimise target leakage, the encoding statistics were treated as split-specific quantities: for each train--test split, encoders were fitted on the training subset only and then applied to the corresponding validation or test subset. A reference/source label was retained only for traceability and was excluded from the predictive feature set because it may encode literature-source-specific bias rather than transferable materials behaviour. After encoding and source-label exclusion, the original feature set contained 22 numeric variables.

To capture physically meaningful interactions, eight additional engineered features were derived from the existing variables: (i) graphene aspect ratio (surface area / thickness), (ii) graphene volume proxy (wt.\% $\times$ thickness $\times$ surface area), (iii) sintering energy (temperature $\times$ time), (iv) sintering intensity (temperature $\times$ pressure), (v) sintering dose (temperature $\times$ time $\times$ pressure), (vi) total additive content (additive 1 + additive 2), (vii) additive ratio (additive 1 / additive 2), and (viii) Si$_3$N$_4$-to-additive ratio (Si$_3$N$_4$ wt.\% / total additive). The complete feature set after engineering consists of 30 numeric features.

The dataset was split into training and test sets using two configurations: 80/20 (66 training, 16 test) and 70/30 (57 training, 25 test). In both cases, a fixed random seed (random\_state = 42) was used to ensure reproducibility. For every split, preprocessing steps that learn parameters from the data, including target encoding and standardisation where applicable, were fitted on the training subset only and transferred unchanged to the held-out test subset. Engineered features were computed from the available input variables without using the hardness target. This workflow was used to keep the held-out test data independent from model fitting and model selection.

The proposed interpretable framework is built upon the RuleFit algorithm \cite{friedman2008predictive}, which combines an ensemble of decision trees with a sparse linear model to produce human-readable prediction rules. In RuleFit, the trees generate candidate rules (conjunctions of feature thresholds), which are then used as binary features in a regularized linear model (Lasso). The final model is a sum of linear terms and weighted rule activations, making every prediction fully traceable.

Hyperparameter tuning of RuleFit was performed by a grid search over \texttt{tree\_size} $\in \{2, 3, 4, 6, 8\}$ and \texttt{max\_rules} $\in \{50, 100, 200, 500\}$. The held-out test set was reserved for final evaluation, and the selected split-specific configuration in the present analysis was \texttt{tree\_size} = 6 and \texttt{max\_rules} = 500, yielding R$^2$ = 0.8535 on the fixed 70/30 evaluation split. This configuration is referred to as \textit{RuleFit (Tuned)} throughout the paper, whereas the untuned configuration (\texttt{tree\_size} = 4, \texttt{max\_rules} = 100) is referred to as \textit{RuleFit (Default)}. For external reuse of the workflow, the same tuning step should be nested within the training data of each split or cross-validation fold so that no test-set information influences model selection.


\subsection{Model Evaluation}  

To assess the performance of the trained machine learning models, multiple evaluation metrics were used to quantify predictive accuracy and error. The evaluation framework was designed to assess the reliability of the models in predicting Vickers hardness. The following statistical metrics were used for performance evaluation:  

\paragraph{Coefficient of Determination ($R^2$)}  
The $R^2$ score measures the proportion of variance in the observed data that is explained by the model’s predictions. A value closer to 1 indicates a strong correlation between predicted and actual values, whereas lower values suggest weaker predictive performance. The $R^2$ metric is particularly useful for understanding the overall explanatory power of the model.  

\paragraph{Root Mean Squared Error (RMSE)}  
RMSE quantifies the model’s predictive accuracy by calculating the square root of the mean squared differences between predicted and actual values. It penalizes larger errors more than smaller ones, making it a suitable metric for evaluating models where significant deviations in predictions are undesirable. RMSE is computed as:  

\begin{equation}
RMSE = \sqrt{\frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} (y_i - \hat{y}_i)^2}
\end{equation}  

where $y_i$ represents the actual values, $\hat{y}_i$ represents the predicted values, and $n$ is the total number of data points.  

In addition to testing set performance, the RMSE has been computed for the training set to evaluate how well the model fits the training data and to identify potential overfitting. The RMSE for the training set reflects the average prediction error during model fitting and provides a baseline for comparison against validation and test performance. A significantly lower RMSE on the training set relative to the test set may indicate overfitting, whereas comparable values suggest generalizability. RMSE is calculated using Equation (1), where predictions $\hat{y}_i$ are generated for the training set compounds.


\paragraph{Adjusted Coefficient of Determination ($R^2_{\text{adj}}$)}  
While the standard $R^2$ score quantifies how well the model captures variance in the data, it does not account for the number of predictors used in the model. Given that the proposed framework incorporates 30 input features (22 original + 8 engineered), the adjusted $R^2$ ($R^2_{\text{adj}}$) is also reported to correct for potential overfitting. Adjusted $R^2$ provides a more accurate assessment of the model’s explanatory power on the training set by penalizing excessive use of features that do not significantly contribute to prediction. It is computed as:

\begin{equation}
R^2_{\text{adj}} = 1 - \left( \frac{(1 - R^2)(n - 1)}{n - p - 1} \right)
\end{equation}

where $n$ is the number of observations, $p$ is the number of predictors (features), and $R^2$ is the unadjusted coefficient of determination. This metric was calculated for the training set to ensure that model complexity is justified by meaningful predictive power.


\subsection{Analysis of the Framework}

\paragraph{Feature clustering}
Hierarchical clustering of all 30 engineered features together with the target variable (Vickers hardness) was performed using Pearson correlation as the similarity measure. The distance between two variables was defined as $d = 1 - |\rho|$, where $\rho$ is the Pearson correlation coefficient. Ward linkage was applied to the resulting distance matrix to produce a dendrogram. Including the target in the clustering allows visual identification of which feature families are most correlated with hardness.

\paragraph{Feature importance}
Feature importance was assessed from two complementary perspectives. First, RuleFit-based importance was computed by aggregating the absolute coefficients of rules in which each feature appeared, weighted by rule activation frequency across the training samples. This measure reflects how often a feature participates in influential rule conditions. Second, SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations) values were computed using the XGBoost model via TreeExplainer to provide attribution across all 25 test-set samples \cite{lundberg2017unified}.

\paragraph{Local interpretability}
Local explanations for five selected test samples were generated using three methods: (i) RuleFit rule activation (which rules fire for a given sample and how they contribute to the prediction), (ii) SHAP values (XGBoost), and (iii) LIME (Local Interpretable Model-agnostic Explanations), which fits a locally weighted linear model around each prediction \cite{ribeiro2016should}. The five samples were selected from the 25-sample test set to represent a range of prediction accuracy: two high-accuracy predictions ($|$error$|$ $<$ 0.15 GPa), two moderate-accuracy predictions ($|$error$|$ $\approx$ 0.85 GPa), and one challenging case ($|$error$|$ = 4.46 GPa).

\paragraph{Statistical significance}
The stability of each model across 30 random splits was evaluated using one-tailed paired Student's $t$-tests (significance level $\alpha = 0.05$). For R$^2$, the null hypothesis is that RuleFit (Tuned) performs no better than the comparison model (one-tailed greater). For MAE and RMSE, the null hypothesis is that RuleFit (Tuned) is no lower than the comparison model (one-tailed less). The critical value for 29 degrees of freedom at $\alpha = 0.05$ (one-tailed) is $t_\text{critical} = 1.699$.

 






\subsection{Comparative Analysis}

The primary reference for comparison is the XGBoost-based model reported by Qadir et al.~\cite{qadir2024predicting}, which provides the strongest published baseline for Vickers hardness prediction on this dataset. That model was trained on the same 82-sample dataset using XGBoost with RandomizedSearchCV hyperparameter optimization (50 iterations, 5-fold cross-validation). Although XGBoost produces high predictive accuracy, it operates as a black-box model: while feature importance scores can be extracted, the model does not generate human-readable decision rules. The proposed framework retains competitive predictive accuracy while providing full interpretability through explicit rule extraction.

The following model configurations are included in the comparison:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{XGBoost (Default Features, Tuned)}: XGBoost with the original 22 features and RandomizedSearchCV optimization (50 iterations, 5-fold CV). This follows the published XGBoost baseline setup.
    \item \textbf{XGBoost (Extra Features, Tuned)}: XGBoost with all 30 features (22 original + 8 engineered) and the same RandomizedSearchCV optimization. This evaluates the contribution of feature engineering.
    \item \textbf{ElasticNet}: Elastic net regularized linear regression (L1 + L2, $\alpha$ = 1.0, $l_1$-ratio = 0.5) applied to standardized 30-feature data.
    \item \textbf{RuleFit (Tuned)}: RuleFit with optimized hyperparameters (\texttt{tree\_size} = 6, \texttt{max\_rules} = 500) trained on 30-feature data.
    \item \textbf{RuleFit (Default)}: RuleFit with default hyperparameters (\texttt{tree\_size} = 4, \texttt{max\_rules} = 100) trained on 30-feature data.
    \item \textbf{DNN Small (32-16)}: A two-hidden-layer multilayer perceptron with 32 and 16 neurons, trained for up to 2000 epochs with early stopping, applied to standardized 30-feature data.
\end{enumerate}

Each model is evaluated on two fixed train-test splits (80/20 and 70/30, both with random\_state = 42) and on 30 different random splits (random\_state = 0, 1, \ldots, 29; test size 30\%) to assess stability. Performance is reported as R$^2$, mean absolute error (MAE, in GPa), and root mean squared error (RMSE, in GPa).

The one-tailed paired $t$-test results (Table~\ref{tab:ttest}) show that at $\alpha = 0.05$ ($t_\text{critical} = 1.699$, df~$= 29$), RuleFit (Tuned) significantly outperforms only Few-Shot KNN across all three metrics (R$^2$: $t = 6.11$, $p < 0.001$; MAE: $t = -6.18$, $p < 0.001$; RMSE: $t = -6.70$, $p < 0.001$). Against XGBoost variants and DNN Small, no statistically significant difference is observed. Linear Regression shows a significant MAE disadvantage relative to RuleFit ($p = 0.039$), but not for R$^2$ or RMSE. Collectively, these results indicate that RuleFit (Tuned) achieves accuracy comparable to the strongest black-box models on this dataset, while providing transparent, human-readable rule extraction. The absence of a statistically significant difference should not be interpreted as a formal equivalence test; it only indicates that the repeated-split analysis did not detect a significant advantage or disadvantage relative to those baselines.

\paragraph{Point P1 (True: 6.5~GPa, RF: 6.544~GPa, XGB: 9.209~GPa).}
This sample is characterised by a high graphene content of 8.0~wt.\%, low sintering pressure (25~MPa), sintering temperature of 1750~$^\circ$C, and a relative density of 98\%. RuleFit activates two rules that drive the prediction toward the low-hardness regime: ``Graphene~$>$~2.0 \& sintering\_intensity~$\leq$~47,375'' (coefficient~$= -2.835$, importance~$= 0.992$) and ``Sintering Temperature~$>$~1612.5 \& Si$_3$N$_4$~$\leq$~87.375'' (coefficient~$= -1.079$). These activations correctly capture the detrimental effect of excess graphene at sub-optimal sintering intensity on densification and hardness. SHAP corroborates this: Sintering Pressure (SHAP~$= -1.896$~GPa) and Graphene content (SHAP~$= -1.116$~GPa) are the two strongest negative contributors relative to the dataset base value of 14.933~GPa. LIME similarly identifies Si$_3$N$_4 \leq$~87.00 ($-2.668$~GPa) and Sintering Pressure~$\leq$~25~MPa ($-2.062$~GPa) as the dominant negative local conditions. Notably, XGBoost overestimates this sample by 2.709~GPa (predicted 9.209~GPa), whereas RuleFit's rule-based constraints correctly suppress the prediction to near the true value. This demonstrates a key interpretability advantage: the activated rules provide an explicit, physically grounded explanation for the low-hardness outcome.

\paragraph{Point P2 (True: 18.3~GPa, RF: 18.438~GPa, XGB: 18.406~GPa).}
This sample contains no graphene (0.0~wt.\%), relative density of 98\%, sintering temperature of 1600~$^\circ$C, and high sintering pressure of 50~MPa. RuleFit activates two positive rules: ``Type of Graphene encoded~$>$~14.236 \& Load~$>$~7.4 \& Density~$>$~87.13'' (coefficient~$= +1.262$) and ``Sintering Pressure~$>$~27.5'' (coefficient~$= +0.652$), correctly associating high pressure and density with elevated hardness. SHAP identifies Density (\texttt{+}0.778~GPa), Si$_3$N$_4$ content (\texttt{+}0.703~GPa), and Sintering Pressure (\texttt{+}0.688~GPa) as the leading positive contributors. LIME confirms: Sintering Pressure~$>$~25~MPa (\texttt{+}1.991~GPa) and Density~$>$~93.84\% (\texttt{+}1.422~GPa) are the strongest local positive conditions. All three methods agree that high sintering pressure and density are the key drivers of elevated hardness in additive-free Si$_3$N$_4$.

\paragraph{Point P3 (True: 13.3~GPa, RF: 14.132~GPa, XGB: 14.432~GPa).}
This sample has no graphene, density 96.52\%, sintering temperature 1700~$^\circ$C, and very low sintering pressure 20~MPa. None of the five extracted RuleFit rules is activated, so the prediction is driven entirely by the model's linear component. SHAP identifies Sintering Pressure (SHAP~$= -1.627$~GPa) as the dominant negative factor, consistent with the low pressure preventing full densification. Graphene absence contributes a positive SHAP value (\texttt{+}0.927~GPa). LIME similarly identifies Sintering Pressure~$\leq$~25~MPa ($-2.093$~GPa) as the strongest downward driver. The moderate overestimation (14.132 vs.\ 13.3~GPa) is consistent with the linear model's tendency to predict toward the training mean when no rule constrains the output. Comparison with XGBoost (14.432~GPa) confirms this is a systematic challenge for both models at very low sintering pressures.

\paragraph{Point P4 (True: 15.5~GPa, RF: 14.654~GPa, XGB: 14.882~GPa).}
This sample contains graphene at 3.175~wt.\%, density 99\%, sintering temperature 1625~$^\circ$C, and sintering pressure 50~MPa. Two RuleFit rules activate with opposing signs: ``Sintering Pressure~$>$~27.5'' (coefficient~$= +0.652$) and ``Density~$>$~87.13 \& Type of Graphene~$\leq$~18.69 \& Load~$\leq$~73.5'' (coefficient~$= -0.799$). The partial cancellation of these rules explains the slight underestimation (14.654 vs.\ 15.5~GPa). SHAP shows Si$_3$N$_4$ content (\texttt{+}0.808~GPa) and Sintering Pressure (\texttt{+}0.718~GPa) as the leading positive contributors, while graphene type encoding acts as a downward factor ($-0.647$~GPa). LIME corroborates: high sintering pressure (\texttt{+}2.059~GPa) and Si$_3$N$_4$ content (\texttt{+}1.377~GPa) are the dominant positive conditions, offset by graphene volume proxy ($-1.039$~GPa) and graphene content ($-0.838$~GPa). The RuleFit rule conflict is explicitly visible and provides a mechanistic explanation for the underestimation.

\paragraph{Point P5 (True: 27.0~GPa, RF: 22.541~GPa, XGB: 21.913~GPa).}
This sample represents the most challenging prediction in the test set, with absolute errors of 4.459~GPa (RuleFit) and 5.087~GPa (XGBoost). Its features include 2.012~wt.\% graphene, near-perfect density of 99.9\%, sintering temperature 1625~$^\circ$C, and sintering pressure 50~MPa. Two positive RuleFit rules activate: ``Type of Graphene encoded~$>$~14.236 \& Load~$>$~7.4 \& Density~$>$~87.13'' (coefficient~$= +1.262$) and ``Sintering Pressure~$>$~27.5'' (coefficient~$= +0.652$). However, SHAP reveals the dominant factor: graphene type encoding (SHAP~$= +3.827$~GPa) is the single largest SHAP magnitude observed across all five samples, driving the XGBoost prediction well above the base value (14.933~GPa) but insufficient to reach 27.0~GPa. LIME identifies Sintering Pressure~$>$~25~MPa (\texttt{+}2.027~GPa) and graphene type encoding~$>$~14.27 (\texttt{+}1.658~GPa) as the strongest positive local conditions. The consistent underestimation by both models (22.5 and 21.9~GPa) indicates a data-driven limitation: this sample's exceptionally high hardness arises from a specific graphene type whose reinforcing effect is under-represented in the 82-sample training set, making precise extrapolation inherently difficult. The transparent rule-based analysis enables researchers to identify this boundary condition explicitly and target additional experiments in this parametric region.

\section{Conclusion}

This study presented an interpretable machine learning framework for predicting the Vickers hardness of graphene-added Si$_3$N$_4$ ceramics using a dataset of 82 experimentally characterised samples. A tuned RuleFit model was developed alongside five baseline model families --- XGBoost with original and engineered features, ElasticNet/linear baselines, Few-Shot KNN, and a small deep neural network --- and evaluated under three experimental protocols: fixed 80/20 and 70/30 train--test splits, and a 30-replicate random-split stability analysis.

On the 70/30 fixed split, RuleFit (Tuned) achieved the highest performance (R$^2 = 0.854$, RMSE~$= 1.955$~GPa, MAE~$= 1.477$~GPa), surpassing both XGBoost variants (R$^2 = 0.817$--$0.835$) and DNN Small (R$^2 = 0.836$). Across 30 random splits, RuleFit demonstrated stable performance (R$^2 = 0.769 \pm 0.089$), comparable to XGBoost (R$^2 = 0.782 \pm 0.096$). One-tailed paired $t$-tests showed that RuleFit statistically significantly outperforms only Few-Shot KNN (all metrics, $p < 0.001$), while performance differences with XGBoost and DNN are not statistically significant ($\alpha = 0.05$, $t_\text{critical} = 1.699$). Linear Regression and DNN exhibited high instability (mean R$^2 < 0$) attributable to the small dataset size.

Feature importance analysis and hierarchical clustering identified three physically meaningful feature families: sintering process parameters, material composition, and graphene morphology descriptors. Graphene content/type, sintering pressure, Si$_3$N$_4$ content, and relative density emerged as recurring predictors across RuleFit and SHAP analyses, aligning with established ceramic processing--property relationships.

Case study analysis of five test samples demonstrated that RuleFit's rule activations provide explicit, physically grounded explanations for individual predictions. In particular, activated rules identified the detrimental role of excess graphene content at sub-optimal sintering intensity (Rule~1: coefficient~$= -2.835$) and the benefit of high sintering pressure (Rule~4: coefficient~$= +0.652$), enabling researchers to trace prediction outcomes to specific processing conditions. Cross-method consistency between RuleFit rule activations, SHAP values, and LIME local explanations further validates the reliability of these interpretations.

The proposed framework demonstrates that interpretable rule-based models can provide competitive accuracy on small ceramic datasets while offering human-readable guidance for experimental design and process optimisation in advanced ceramic manufacturing. The main limitations are the small dataset size, literature-source heterogeneity, the inclusion of post-sintering descriptors such as density and phase composition, the need for strictly leakage-free encoding/tuning, and the absence of external validation on newly fabricated samples. Future work should therefore separate pre-synthesis and post-synthesis prediction tasks, perform source-label ablation, and validate the rules experimentally in the high-hardness regions identified by the model.
